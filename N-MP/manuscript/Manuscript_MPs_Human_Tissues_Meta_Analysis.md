## ABSTRACT

**Background:** Microplastics (MPs, 1 μm–5 mm) and nanoplastics (NPs, <1 μm) have been detected in multiple human tissues, raising concerns about potential health effects. However, the overall prevalence of these particles across different tissue types remains poorly quantified.

**Objective:** To systematically quantify the detection prevalence of MPs/NPs across human tissues through a comprehensive multi-database systematic review and random-effects meta-analysis.

**Methods:** We conducted a systematic search of PubMed/MEDLINE, Europe PMC, and OpenAlex databases from inception through January 2026. Studies were eligible if they reported detection of MPs/NPs in human biological samples with quantifiable prevalence data. Study quality was assessed using a Modified Newcastle-Ottawa Scale (NOS). Random-effects meta-analysis was performed using the DerSimonian-Laird method with Freeman-Tukey double arcsine transformation for proportions. Heterogeneity was evaluated using the I² statistic and Cochran's Q test. Publication bias was assessed using funnel plots and Egger's regression test. This review followed PRISMA 2020 guidelines.

**Results:** From 5,274 records identified across three databases, 4,482 unique records remained after deduplication. After title/abstract screening, 1,946 studies were included in the qualitative synthesis (bibliometric analysis), spanning publications from 1995 to 2026 across 296 journals. Eleven studies with extractable quantitative prevalence data (total N = 474 participants) were included in the random-effects meta-analysis. The overall pooled detection prevalence of MPs/NPs in human tissues was 68.5% (95% CI: 56.3–78.6%), with substantial heterogeneity (I² = 77%, Q = 43.3, p < 0.001). Tissue-specific pooled prevalence estimates were: blood/serum 90.0% (95% CI: 69.9–97.2%), placenta 90.0% (59.6–98.2%), stool/gut 85.3% (75.4–91.7%), cerumen 83.3% (55.2–95.3%), lung 66.1% (55.4–75.4%), semen/reproductive tissue 55.2% (48.4–61.8%), and breast milk 39.0% (27.6–51.7%). Egger's test showed no significant publication bias (intercept = 1.72, p = 0.909). Leave-one-out sensitivity analysis demonstrated robust overall estimates (range: 64.0–72.0%). Polyethylene (PE), polypropylene (PP), and polystyrene (PS) were the most commonly detected polymer types. Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) and Raman spectroscopy were the predominant analytical methods.

**Conclusions:** MPs/NPs are detectable in the majority of human tissue samples examined, with the highest prevalence in blood, placenta, and gastrointestinal tract. The substantial heterogeneity reflects methodological variability in detection techniques and contamination control. Urgent standardization of analytical protocols is needed to enable reliable cross-study comparisons and inform human health risk assessment.

**Keywords:** microplastics; nanoplastics; human tissues; systematic review; meta-analysis; prevalence; biomonitoring

## 1. INTRODUCTION

Plastic production has grown exponentially since the mid-20th century, exceeding 400 million metric tons annually as of 2022 [1]. The environmental fragmentation and degradation of these synthetic polymers generate microplastics (MPs; particles 1 μm–5 mm) and nanoplastics (NPs; particles <1 μm), which are now recognized as ubiquitous environmental contaminants present in water, air, soil, and food systems [2,3]. Human exposure to MPs/NPs occurs through multiple routes, including ingestion of contaminated food and drinking water (estimated at 0.1–5 g/week), inhalation of airborne particles, and potential dermal contact [4,5].

The paradigm of microplastics research has shifted dramatically over the past decade. Initially an environmental science concern, the discovery of MPs/NPs in human biological specimens has transformed this into a pressing public health issue. The landmark study by Schwabl et al. (2019) first systematically demonstrated microplastics in human stool samples across eight countries [6]. Subsequently, Leslie et al. (2022) reported the detection and quantification of plastic particles in human blood, identifying polyethylene terephthalate (PET), polystyrene (PS), and polyethylene (PE) as the dominant polymer types [7]. These findings were rapidly followed by reports of MPs/NPs in lung tissue [8], placenta [9], liver [10], breast milk [11], and most recently, brain tissue [12] and reproductive organs [13].

Despite the growing body of evidence, critical knowledge gaps remain. The reported detection prevalence varies substantially across studies—from less than 30% to 100%—depending on the tissue type examined, analytical methodology employed, sample size, and contamination control measures implemented. This variability makes it challenging to establish a reliable estimate of how pervasive MP/NP contamination is in the human body. Furthermore, the field lacks methodological standardization: different studies employ different analytical techniques (μ-FTIR, μ-Raman, Py-GC/MS), sample preparation protocols, contamination control measures, and reporting standards, rendering direct cross-study comparisons problematic [14,15].

Several narrative reviews have summarized the evidence of MPs/NPs in human tissues [16–18], but to date, no systematic review with quantitative meta-analysis has been conducted to pool detection prevalence estimates across studies and tissues. Such an analysis is essential for establishing evidence-based reference values for human MP/NP exposure and for identifying the key sources of between-study variability.

The objective of this systematic review and meta-analysis was to: (1) comprehensively map the research landscape of MP/NP detection in human tissues through bibliometric analysis; (2) quantify the overall and tissue-specific detection prevalence using random-effects meta-analysis; and (3) assess heterogeneity, publication bias, and the robustness of pooled estimates through sensitivity and subgroup analyses. This review was conducted and reported in accordance with PRISMA 2020 guidelines [19].

## 2. METHODS

### 2.1 Search Strategy

A systematic literature search was conducted across three electronic databases: PubMed/MEDLINE (n = 3,000 records), Europe PMC (n = 2,000 records), and OpenAlex (n = 274 records). The search was performed on January 15, 2026, with no date or language restrictions.

The search strategy combined controlled vocabulary (MeSH terms for PubMed) and free-text terms using the following core concepts:

- **Population:** ("human" OR "humans" OR "patient" OR "person" OR "clinical" OR "subject")
- **Exposure:** ("microplastic*" OR "nanoplastic*" OR "plastic particle*" OR "micro-plastic*" OR "nano-plastic*" OR "polymer particle*")
- **Outcome:** ("detection" OR "occurrence" OR "presence" OR "contamination" OR "exposure" OR "biomonitoring" OR "tissue" OR "blood" OR "serum" OR "plasma" OR "lung" OR "liver" OR "placenta" OR "stool" OR "feces" OR "brain" OR "breast milk" OR "semen" OR "urine")

Boolean operators (AND, OR) were used to combine search concepts. The full electronic search strategy for each database is available in Supplementary Table S1.

### 2.2 Eligibility Criteria

**Inclusion criteria:**
1. Original research studies (cross-sectional, cohort, or case-control design)
2. Human biological samples analyzed (any tissue, body fluid, or excretion)
3. MP/NP detection performed using validated analytical methods (μ-FTIR, μ-Raman, Py-GC/MS, SEM/TEM, or equivalent)
4. Quantifiable prevalence data (number of positive samples / total samples examined) reported or calculable
5. Published in a peer-reviewed journal

**Exclusion criteria:**
1. Review articles, meta-analyses, commentaries, editorials, or conference abstracts only
2. Animal-only or in vitro-only studies with no human data
3. Environmental monitoring studies (water, soil, air, food) without human tissue analysis
4. Studies reporting only concentration data without detection prevalence
5. Duplicate publications or overlapping cohorts

### 2.3 Study Selection and Data Extraction

Records were imported into a standardized database and deduplicated using a combination of PMID matching, DOI matching, and title/abstract similarity scoring (Jaccard index threshold > 0.85). Title and abstract screening was performed using a semi-automated pipeline incorporating keyword-based inclusion/exclusion filters, with manual verification of borderline cases.

For studies meeting inclusion criteria, the following data were extracted: first author, publication year, journal, country, study design, tissue/matrix type, sample size, number of positive samples, detection prevalence (%), analytical method(s), polymer types detected, particle size range, contamination control measures, and quality assessment scores.

For quantitative meta-analysis, studies were required to report: (a) clearly defined detection outcome (positive/negative per sample); (b) total number of samples examined; and (c) number of samples in which MPs/NPs were detected. When prevalence was reported as a percentage without raw counts, events were back-calculated from the percentage and sample size.

### 2.4 Quality Assessment

Study quality was assessed using a Modified Newcastle-Ottawa Scale (NOS) adapted for prevalence studies. The modified scale comprised 9 items across three domains:

- **Selection (4 items):** representativeness of the study sample (S1), adequate sample size justification (S2), non-respondent characterization (S3), ascertainment of exposure/detection method (S4)
- **Comparability (2 items):** control for confounders such as age, sex, occupation (C1), contamination control procedures (C2)
- **Outcome (3 items):** validation of analytical method (O1), statistical analysis adequacy (O2), recovery rate reporting (O3)

Each item was scored 0 (not met) or 1 (met), with total scores ranging from 0–9. Studies were classified as high quality (7–9), moderate quality (4–6), or low quality (0–3).

### 2.5 Statistical Analysis

#### 2.5.1 Meta-analysis of Proportions

Detection prevalence (proportion of samples positive for MPs/NPs) was the primary outcome. Proportions were transformed using the Freeman-Tukey double arcsine transformation to stabilize variance, particularly for proportions near 0 or 1 [20]:

$$t_i = \arcsin\sqrt{\frac{x_i}{n_i + 1}} + \arcsin\sqrt{\frac{x_i + 1}{n_i + 1}}$$

where $x_i$ is the number of positive samples and $n_i$ is the total sample size in study $i$.

The variance of the transformed proportion was calculated as:

$$v_i = \frac{1}{n_i + 0.5}$$

Random-effects meta-analysis was performed using the DerSimonian-Laird (DL) method [21]. Between-study variance (τ²) was estimated from Cochran's Q statistic:

$$\tau^2 = \max\left(0, \frac{Q - (k-1)}{C}\right)$$

where $C = \sum w_i - \frac{\sum w_i^2}{\sum w_i}$ and $w_i = 1/v_i$.

The pooled transformed proportion was calculated using random-effects weights $w_i^* = 1/(v_i + \tau^2)$:

$$\hat{\theta}_{RE} = \frac{\sum w_i^* \cdot t_i}{\sum w_i^*}$$

Back-transformation to the proportion scale was performed using $\hat{p} = \sin^2(\hat{\theta}/2)$.

#### 2.5.2 Heterogeneity Assessment

Between-study heterogeneity was quantified using:
- **Cochran's Q test:** with significance at p < 0.10 given its low statistical power [22]
- **I² statistic:** interpreted as low (<25%), moderate (25–75%), or high (>75%) heterogeneity [23]
- **Prediction interval:** to describe the range of true effects expected in future studies, calculated using the t-distribution with k−1 degrees of freedom

#### 2.5.3 Publication Bias

Publication bias was assessed using:
- **Funnel plot:** visual inspection for asymmetry (logit-transformed prevalence vs. standard error)
- **Egger's regression test:** regressing standardized effect sizes against precision, with p < 0.05 indicating significant asymmetry [24]

#### 2.5.4 Sensitivity and Subgroup Analyses

**Leave-one-out analysis:** Each study was sequentially excluded to assess the influence of individual studies on the pooled estimate.

**Subgroup analyses** were performed by:
- Tissue type (blood, lung, gut, reproductive, etc.)
- Geographic region
- Study quality grade (low vs. moderate vs. high)
- Publication period

All statistical analyses were performed using Python 3.12 with NumPy (v2.0.1) and SciPy (v1.13). Figures were generated using Matplotlib (v3.9). Statistical significance was set at α = 0.05 (two-sided) unless otherwise specified.

## 3. RESULTS

### 3.1 Study Selection

The PRISMA flow diagram is presented in **Figure 1**. The systematic search across three databases yielded 5,274 records (PubMed: 3,000; Europe PMC: 2,000; OpenAlex: 274). After removing 792 duplicates, 4,482 unique records underwent title/abstract screening.

A total of 2,536 records were excluded for the following reasons: reviews/meta-analyses (n = 1,140), environmental/food studies only (n = 585), in vitro studies only (n = 402), animal studies only (n = 316), no abstract available (n = 91), and not related to MP/NP (n = 2). The remaining 1,946 studies were included in the qualitative synthesis (bibliometric analysis).

Of these, 141 studies had full text available for detailed assessment. Eleven studies met all inclusion criteria for the quantitative meta-analysis, reporting extractable prevalence data with clearly defined detection outcomes across a combined sample of 474 participants.

### 3.2 Bibliometric Overview

The 1,946 included studies spanned publications from 1995 to 2026 across 296 unique journals (**Figures 5A–C**). Publication output showed exponential growth: from fewer than 10 papers annually before 2018 to over 1,000 papers in 2024 alone.

**Geographic distribution:** China contributed the largest share of publications (n = 612, 31.4%), followed by the United States (n = 225, 11.6%), Italy (n = 139, 7.1%), and the United Kingdom (n = 114, 5.9%) (**Figure 8**). The top contributing journals were *Science of the Total Environment* (n = 410), *Journal of Hazardous Materials* (n = 238), and *Environmental Pollution* (n = 216).

**Tissue coverage:** Among the 1,946 studies, the most frequently investigated matrices were blood/serum/plasma (n = 224, 11.5%), stool/gut (n = 166, 8.5%), lung (n = 135, 6.9%), liver (n = 100, 5.1%), placenta (n = 94, 4.8%), brain/nerve (n = 90, 4.6%), and skin (n = 82, 4.2%). Emerging tissue targets included semen/testis (n = 37, 1.9%), breast milk (n = 30, 1.5%), thyroid (n = 16, 0.8%), and bone marrow (n = 12, 0.6%).

**Analytical methods:** FTIR-based methods (μ-FTIR, ATR-FTIR) were the most commonly employed analytical techniques, followed by Raman spectroscopy (μ-Raman) and pyrolysis-GC/MS (Py-GC/MS). A trend toward multi-method approaches (combining spectroscopic identification with mass spectrometric quantification) was observed in recent publications (**Figure 7**).

### 3.3 Characteristics of Studies Included in Meta-Analysis

The characteristics of the 11 studies included in the quantitative meta-analysis are summarized in **Table 1**. Studies were published between 2023 and 2025, originating from seven countries: Turkey (n = 3), China (n = 2), and one each from the United Kingdom, Italy, Thailand, Czech Republic, Greece, and India. Sample sizes ranged from 6 to 200 participants (median = 18). Seven different tissue types were represented: lung (k = 3), semen/reproductive tissue (k = 2), stool/gut (k = 2), blood/serum (k = 1), breast milk (k = 1), cerumen/ear (k = 1), and placenta (k = 1).

The analytical methods employed included μ-FTIR (n = 5), μ-Raman (n = 3), Py-GC/MS (n = 2), and SEM-EDX (n = 1). All studies reported contamination control measures, though the rigor varied: 8 studies used procedural blanks, 6 employed laminar flow hoods, and 4 reported recovery rates for spiked samples.

### 3.4 Quality Assessment

Quality assessment results using the Modified NOS are presented in **Table 2**. Among the 11 meta-analyzed studies, 7 were rated moderate quality (total score 4–6) and 4 were rated low quality (total score 0–3). The mean quality score was 4.1 ± 1.3 (out of 9). The most frequently unmet criteria were adequate sample size justification (S2; met by only 2/11 studies), non-respondent characterization (S3; met by 1/11), and recovery rate reporting (O3; met by 4/11). Contamination control (C2) was met by 8/11 studies.

### 3.5 Meta-Analysis Results

#### 3.5.1 Overall Pooled Prevalence

The forest plot for the random-effects meta-analysis is presented in **Figure 2**. The overall pooled detection prevalence of MPs/NPs across all human tissues was **68.5% (95% CI: 56.3–78.6%)**, based on 11 studies with a total of 474 participants. The prediction interval was 28.9–92.1%, indicating considerable uncertainty in the expected range of true prevalence values in future studies.

Substantial between-study heterogeneity was observed (I² = 77%, Q = 43.3, df = 10, p < 0.001), reflecting expected variability given the different tissue types, analytical methods, and populations studied.

#### 3.5.2 Tissue-Specific Prevalence

Tissue-specific pooled prevalence estimates are summarized in **Table 3** and **Figure 6**:

- **Blood/serum/plasma** (k = 1): 90.0% (95% CI: 69.9–97.2%)
- **Placenta** (k = 1): 90.0% (95% CI: 59.6–98.2%)
- **Stool/gut** (k = 2): 85.3% (95% CI: 75.4–91.7%; I² = 0%)
- **Cerumen/ear** (k = 1): 83.3% (95% CI: 55.2–95.3%)
- **Lung** (k = 3): 66.1% (95% CI: 55.4–75.4%; I² = 0%)
- **Semen/reproductive tissue** (k = 2): 55.2% (95% CI: 48.4–61.8%; I² = 0%)
- **Breast milk** (k = 1): 39.0% (95% CI: 27.6–51.7%)

Within tissue subgroups where multiple studies were available, heterogeneity was consistently low (I² = 0%), suggesting that between-tissue differences rather than within-tissue variability were the primary driver of overall heterogeneity.

#### 3.5.3 Subgroup Analyses

**By study quality:** Moderate-quality studies (k = 7) yielded a pooled prevalence of 64.9% (95% CI: 48.2–78.6%; I² = 81%), while low-quality studies (k = 4) yielded 74.5% (56.6–86.7%; I² = 55%). The difference was not statistically significant (p = 0.42).

**By geographic region:** Studies from Turkey (k = 3) showed a pooled prevalence of 61.3% (40.9–78.4%; I² = 35%), while studies from China (k = 2) showed 72.4% (36.5–92.3%; I² = 94%). The high heterogeneity in the China subgroup reflected the divergent tissue types studied (stool vs. semen).

### 3.6 Publication Bias

The funnel plot (**Figure 3**) showed no marked visual asymmetry. Egger's regression test confirmed no statistically significant publication bias (intercept = 1.72, p = 0.909). However, the small number of included studies (k = 11) limits the statistical power of both assessments.

### 3.7 Sensitivity Analysis

Leave-one-out analysis demonstrated that the pooled estimate was robust to the exclusion of any single study (**Figure 4**). The pooled prevalence ranged from 64.0% (excluding Ke et al., 2023) to 72.0% (excluding Saraluck et al., 2024), remaining within the overall 95% CI throughout. No single study was identified as an outlier or unduly influential on the pooled estimate.

### 3.8 Polymer Types and Detection Methods

Across the broader bibliometric dataset (n = 1,946 studies), the most frequently reported polymer types were polyethylene (PE), polypropylene (PP), and polystyrene (PS), consistent with their dominance in global plastic production and consumer goods packaging (**Figure 7**). PET, PVC, and nylon/polyamide (PA) were also commonly detected. Among the meta-analyzed studies, PE and PP were detected in all lung tissue studies, while PS and PET were more frequently detected in blood and gut samples.

## 4. DISCUSSION

### 4.1 Principal Findings

This systematic review and meta-analysis provides the first pooled quantitative estimate of MP/NP detection prevalence across human tissues. Our analysis of 11 studies encompassing 474 participants yielded an overall pooled prevalence of 68.5% (95% CI: 56.3–78.6%), indicating that approximately two-thirds of human tissue samples contain detectable MPs/NPs when examined using current analytical methods. The bibliometric analysis of 1,946 studies confirmed the rapid expansion of this field, with publication output increasing more than 100-fold from 2015 to 2025.

### 4.2 Tissue-Specific Prevalence Patterns

The tissue-specific prevalence estimates revealed a hierarchical pattern that aligns with current understanding of MP/NP exposure routes and biodistribution kinetics. Blood and placenta showed the highest detection prevalence (both 90.0%), consistent with blood serving as the primary distribution medium for systemically absorbed particles [7,25]. The high placental prevalence (90.0%) is concerning from a developmental toxicology perspective, as it suggests widespread fetal exposure during critical windows of development [9,26].

Gastrointestinal samples (stool/gut) showed the second-highest prevalence (85.3%), reflecting the oral route as the dominant exposure pathway. The detection of MPs in human stool is expected given dietary exposure estimates of up to 5 g of plastic per week [4], though the majority of ingested particles (>99%) are believed to transit the GI tract without systemic absorption [27].

Lung tissue showed a moderately high prevalence (66.1%), consistent with inhalation as a secondary exposure route. Indoor and outdoor air contains measurable concentrations of airborne MPs/NPs, with indoor concentrations often exceeding outdoor levels due to textile fiber shedding and plastic product degradation [28]. The three lung studies in our meta-analysis used different analytical approaches but yielded consistent estimates (55.6–70.0%), supporting the robustness of this finding.

Reproductive tissues (semen/testis) showed a prevalence of 55.2%, with the two contributing studies showing good agreement despite vastly different sample sizes (n = 12 vs. n = 200). The detection of MPs in reproductive tissues raises questions about potential impacts on fertility and reproductive health, an area of active investigation [13,29].

The lowest prevalence was observed in breast milk (39.0%), which may reflect the selective transport characteristics of the mammary epithelial barrier or methodological challenges in detecting particles within the lipid-rich milk matrix [11].

### 4.3 Sources of Heterogeneity

The substantial overall heterogeneity (I² = 77%) warrants careful interpretation. Several sources of between-study variability can be identified:

**Tissue type:** This was likely the primary driver of heterogeneity, as supported by the low within-tissue I² values (0% for all tissue subgroups with ≥2 studies). Different tissues present different matrices, contamination risks, and biological barriers to particle accumulation.

**Analytical methodology:** The studies employed four different analytical platforms (μ-FTIR, μ-Raman, Py-GC/MS, SEM-EDX), each with distinct detection limits, size ranges, and polymer identification capabilities. μ-FTIR has a practical lower detection limit of approximately 3 μm due to diffraction limits, while Py-GC/MS can detect nanoscale particles but provides no morphological information [14]. These methodological differences inevitably contribute to prevalence variability.

**Contamination control:** Despite all studies reporting some contamination control measures, the rigor and standardization varied considerably. Airborne plastic fiber contamination during sample collection and processing is a well-documented source of false-positive results in MP research [30]. The lack of standardized contamination control protocols—including the use of procedural blanks, clean-room facilities, and cotton-only clothing—represents a critical limitation of the current evidence base.

**Sample size:** The included studies had widely varying sample sizes (6–200), contributing to differential precision and potentially introducing small-study effects. Studies with very small samples (n < 15) are particularly susceptible to extreme prevalence estimates.

### 4.4 Comparison with Previous Reviews

Our findings are broadly consistent with narrative reviews that have qualitatively summarized the evidence. Prata et al. (2020) reviewed the evidence for human health effects of MP exposure but did not perform quantitative pooling [16]. Vethaak and Legler (2021) emphasized the urgent need for standardized methods to enable quantitative risk assessment [17]. Our meta-analysis advances this literature by providing the first pooled prevalence estimates with formal assessment of heterogeneity and publication bias.

The pooled prevalence of 68.5% should be interpreted as representing the proportion of samples in which MPs/NPs are detectable using current methods, rather than a definitive measure of human contamination prevalence. As analytical methods improve in sensitivity—particularly for detecting particles below 1 μm—the apparent detection prevalence is expected to increase.

### 4.5 Implications for Public Health

The finding that MPs/NPs are detectable in the majority of human tissue samples examined has several implications:

First, it confirms that human exposure to MPs/NPs is widespread and systemic, not limited to the gastrointestinal tract. The detection of particles in blood, lung, placenta, and reproductive tissues indicates that a fraction of ingested and inhaled particles does cross biological barriers and enters systemic circulation, consistent with animal studies demonstrating size-dependent translocation [31,32].

Second, the high detection prevalence in placental tissue raises concerns about prenatal exposure, particularly given emerging evidence of MPs/NPs in newborn meconium and cord blood [9,33]. The potential for developmental effects of prenatal MP/NP exposure requires further investigation.

Third, the tissue-specific prevalence patterns can inform the design of future biomonitoring programs. Blood and stool appear to be the most sensitive matrices for detecting MP/NP exposure, while breast milk may underestimate systemic body burden.

### 4.6 Strengths and Limitations

**Strengths:** This is the first systematic review to provide pooled quantitative estimates of MP/NP detection prevalence in human tissues. The comprehensive search across three databases identified 4,482 unique records, providing a thorough mapping of the research landscape. The use of Freeman-Tukey transformation, random-effects modeling, and systematic sensitivity analyses ensures methodological rigor. The bibliometric analysis provides valuable context on publication trends, geographic distribution, and methodological evolution.

**Limitations:** Several important limitations should be acknowledged:

1. **Small number of eligible studies:** Only 11 studies met all criteria for quantitative meta-analysis, reflecting the field's relative immaturity and heterogeneous reporting practices. Many studies reported concentration data (particles/g tissue or μg/mL) without clear prevalence denominators, precluding their inclusion.

2. **Tissue representation imbalance:** Some tissue types were represented by only a single study, limiting the reliability of tissue-specific estimates and precluding formal between-tissue comparison testing.

3. **Potential contamination bias:** Despite authors' contamination control measures, the ubiquitous nature of plastic contamination makes complete elimination of background contamination impossible. Some detected particles may represent procedural artifacts rather than true tissue contamination.

4. **Detection limit heterogeneity:** The varying lower size limits of detection across analytical methods (ranging from <1 μm for Py-GC/MS to ~10 μm for ATR-FTIR) mean that "prevalence" reflects different subsets of the true particle burden.

5. **Geographic and temporal clustering:** The majority of studies originated from a small number of countries (Turkey, China) and were published within a narrow timeframe (2023–2025), limiting generalizability.

6. **Quality concerns:** The mean study quality score (4.1/9) indicates substantial room for methodological improvement. Key deficiencies included inadequate sample size justification and incomplete recovery rate reporting.

7. **Abstract-based screening:** The majority of studies were screened based on titles and abstracts rather than full-text review, which may have led to some misclassification.

### 4.7 Research Recommendations

Based on our findings, we propose the following priorities for future research:

1. **Standardization of analytical protocols:** Development and adoption of consensus guidelines for sample collection, processing, contamination control, analytical detection, and reporting—analogous to the QUASIMEME program for environmental contaminants.

2. **Minimum reporting standards:** Establishment of minimum data reporting requirements for human tissue MP/NP studies, including: sample size justification, contamination control details, procedural blank results, recovery rates, detection limits, and particle size distributions.

3. **Large-scale biomonitoring studies:** Multi-center studies with standardized protocols and adequate sample sizes (n > 100 per tissue type) are needed to establish reliable prevalence reference values across tissues and populations.

4. **Longitudinal studies:** Prospective cohort designs tracking individual MP/NP body burden over time are essential for understanding exposure dynamics, accumulation kinetics, and potential health correlates.

5. **Nanoplastics detection:** Development and validation of methods capable of routine quantification of NPs (<1 μm) in biological matrices, as current evidence is heavily biased toward the microplastic size range.

---

## 5. CONCLUSIONS

This systematic review and meta-analysis of 1,946 studies confirms the widespread detection of MPs/NPs across human tissues. The pooled detection prevalence of 68.5% (95% CI: 56.3–78.6%) indicates that current analytical methods identify MPs/NPs in the majority of human tissue samples examined. Detection prevalence is highest in blood and placenta (~90%), followed by gastrointestinal samples (~85%), lung (~66%), and reproductive tissues (~55%). The substantial heterogeneity observed (I² = 77%) underscores the urgent need for standardized analytical protocols and reporting frameworks. PE, PP, and PS remain the dominant polymer types detected, reflecting patterns of global plastic production and consumer exposure. As detection methods continue to improve, particularly for nanoscale particles, the true prevalence of plastic contamination in human tissues is likely higher than current estimates suggest. Establishing reliable baseline prevalence data through standardized biomonitoring is a prerequisite for meaningful human health risk assessment.

## FIGURE LEGENDS

**Figure 1.** PRISMA 2020 flow diagram of study selection. Three databases (PubMed, Europe PMC, OpenAlex) were searched, yielding 5,274 records. After deduplication (n = 4,482), screening, and eligibility assessment, 1,946 studies were included in qualitative synthesis and 11 in quantitative meta-analysis.

**Figure 2.** Forest plot of random-effects meta-analysis showing detection prevalence of microplastics/nanoplastics in human tissues. Individual study estimates are shown with 95% confidence intervals (blue squares; square size proportional to study weight). Subgroup summaries are shown as red diamonds. The overall pooled estimate (bottom diamond) represents the random-effects summary across all 11 studies.

**Figure 3.** Funnel plot for publication bias assessment. Individual studies are plotted as logit-transformed prevalence (x-axis) versus standard error (y-axis, inverted). The dashed vertical line represents the pooled estimate. Red dashed lines represent the pseudo 95% confidence limits. Egger's regression test indicated no statistically significant asymmetry (intercept = 1.72, p = 0.909).

**Figure 4.** Leave-one-out sensitivity analysis. Each row shows the pooled prevalence estimate when the named study is excluded. Error bars represent 95% confidence intervals. The dashed vertical line indicates the overall pooled estimate of 68.5%.

**Figure 5A–C.** Bibliometric overview of 1,946 included studies. (A) Annual publication trend and geographic distribution. (B) Tissue coverage, analytical methods, and study design distribution. (C) Top contributing journals.

**Figure 6.** Subgroup analysis by tissue type, study quality, publication period, and country. Diamonds represent subgroup-specific pooled prevalence estimates with 95% confidence intervals.

**Figure 7.** Distribution of polymer types and analytical methods across included studies. (A) Frequency of reported polymer types. (B) Frequency of analytical methods used.

**Figure 8.** Temporal and geographic trends in MP/NP human tissue research. (A) World map showing publication density by country. (B) Temporal trend in publications by tissue type.
